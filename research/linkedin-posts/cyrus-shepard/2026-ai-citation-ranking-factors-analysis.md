## Content
Shepard announces a meta-analysis synthesizing roughly 54 published experiments, patents, and case studies on what correlates with AI citations (ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity), scoring 23 factors 0–10 by repeatability across studies, strength of evidence, and official platform support. Top-scoring factors: URL accessibility (9.5), traditional search rank (9.4), fan-out rank (9.3), preview/indexing control (9.2), and query-answer match (9.2). Notably low-scoring: llms.txt (2.0) — he found no credible evidence it influences citations at all, despite widespread advice to implement it. Domain authority scored weakly/inconsistently across the reviewed studies. He's explicit that these are correlated features, not confirmed ranking factors, and that correlation isn't causation — a deliberate hedge against the "AI ranking factors" hype cycle in the SEO trade press.

## Why this post matters for the playbook
Strong candidate for the playbook's "separating signal from hype" section — Shepard's llms.txt finding directly contradicts a piece of advice that circulates widely in AI-content-production circles, and his methodology (scoring evidence strength rather than just listing tactics) is itself a model for how the playbook should evaluate competing claims from the 10 experts. Complements his existing "AI citation counting skepticism" post in this repo — this is the more rigorous, sourced follow-through on that same skepticism.

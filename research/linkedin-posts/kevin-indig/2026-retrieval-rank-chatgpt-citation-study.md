## Content
Indig partnered with AirOps on a large-scale study of roughly 815,000 query-and-page pairs across nearly 17,000 queries in 10 industries, looking at what actually predicts whether a page gets cited by ChatGPT. Three findings anchor the post:

1. Retrieval rank is the dominant signal — a page retrieved in position 1 has a 58% chance of being cited, dropping to 14% by position 10.
2. Comprehensiveness doesn't win outright — pages covering only 26–50% of a query's "fan-out" sub-questions get cited more often than pages attempting to cover 100% of them.
3. Domain authority has effectively no predictive power — always-cited pages in the dataset had lower domain authority scores than never-cited pages.

A companion point from the same post/thread: headline framing matters — pages with headlines that directly answer the question get cited around 41% of the time, versus 29% for pages with only loosely related headlines.

## Why this post matters for the playbook
Directly challenges the "write the most comprehensive guide possible" instinct that AI-content teams often default to — the data suggests over-covering a topic can hurt citation odds relative to a tightly scoped page that ranks/retrieves well. Also undercuts domain-authority-as-moat thinking for AI search specifically, which is a recurring tension point across several of the 10 experts (compare to Cyrus Shepard's citation-counting skepticism post already in this repo).

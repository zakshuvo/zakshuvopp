Long breaks down Google research describing S-CTS ("Scalable Cluster Termination System") as a system for detecting spam at the cluster level — i.e., identifying when multiple accounts or properties are coordinating to publish mass-produced AI-generated content. He explains S-BERT as a supporting technique: using sentence-level embeddings to detect an "AI footprint" when snippets of text show unusually high similarity patterns consistent with AI generation, which can trigger inclusion in the broader S-CTS detection system. He connects this to recently observed ranking volatility, suggesting Google is actively refining AI-content detection.

## Why this post matters for the playbook

Directly relevant to any playbook guidance on production risk: if Google is using embedding-similarity signals to detect coordinated, mass-produced AI content at the cluster level, that's a strong argument against high-volume, low-differentiation AI content strategies — reinforcing points made independently by Kevin Indig (commodity content) and Amanda Natividad's circle (content-at-scale risk).
